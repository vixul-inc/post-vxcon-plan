Theme 1 — Building the AI-First Foundation

First of 4 theme posts. Provocation-first. References VixulCon talks without being a recap.
Draft post
At VixulCon, three talks tackled the same foundational question: what does it actually mean to build an AI-first services company — not just use AI in one? Most services companies are using AI to do what they already do, faster. That'll improve margins short-term. But the companies that will be worth acquiring in 3 years aren't optimizing their current delivery model with AI. They're rebuilding what delivery means. The difference shows up in how you staff, how you price, and whether your clients can ever outgrow you. Three VixulCon sessions went deep on this: Aater Suleman (Vixul) on building AI-first services companies — customization at scale only works when delivery velocity and margins move together. Uttam Kumaran & Robert Tseng (Brainforge AI) on building an AI-native agency — when AI is the foundation, your staffing model, pricing, and client expectations all need rebuilding from scratch. Mannan Amin (Tintash) on why AI transformation fails without Workflow-User Fit — without it, AI investment is theatre. The through-line: if everyone rebuilds on AI, what's actually defensible? The room didn't fully answer that. Neither has anyone else — yet. #ServiceAsSoftware #AIServices #VixulCon

Tension Map — where the room disagreed
From Vixul page To Public — team reposts Morning — 9am CST ⚡ Marketing team
›
Controversy drives comments. Best engagement post of the 10 days. Ends with a question to invite replies.
Draft post
VixulCon 2026 was an invite-only gathering of B2B services founders in Austin earlier this month. Two focus groups. Sixteen tables. One theme: AI and what it actually does to how services firms get built, priced, and run. The room wasn't in agreement. Three clean splits, where smart founders lined up on opposite sides with real evidence: 1. Do services margins go up under AI, or leak to the client? One table: AI compresses delivery cost, judgment holds premium — services margins are up. Firms with both product and services lines should treat services as the margin engine. Another: the gains flow through to clients as discount demands. Percentage margin improves; dollar margin doesn't. Both cited live engagements. Direct contradiction. 2. Stay AI-agnostic, or commit to a winning stack? Agnostic camp: no provider has durable superiority. Wrap services around the client's preferred stack and stay flexible — optionality is worth its cost. Horse-pickers: the firms that committed early to OpenAI and switched fast to Anthropic when capability flipped beat the firms that straddled and moved slower. Optionality is what makes you late. Both showed concrete results. Both can't be right. 3. Can judgment be priced explicitly, or does it only sell when bundled? One side: a retainer for scoping and architectural judgment is sellable on its own — clients will pay a premium for de-risking decisions before the build. The other: clients won't pay for an abstract deliverable. Judgment has to ride inside a tool wrapper or a recurring retainer, or it's just T&M with extra steps. Nobody could point to a live example of pure judgment-pricing working. Nobody could falsify the case for it either. Neither side was wrong on any of these. The room didn't converge because the underlying questions are open — and anyone telling you AI services has a settled playbook hasn't been in the rooms where it's actually being figured out. Where do you land? #ServiceAsSoftware #AIServices #VixulCon

The question VixulCon didn't answer
From Vixul page To Public — team reposts Morning — 9am CST ⚡ Marketing team
›
Intellectually honest. Surfaces unresolved threads. Very shareable — founders will tag each other.
Draft post
Some questions got answered at VixulCon. These three didn't. We ran two focus groups in Austin earlier this month — sixteen tables of B2B services founders working through the hardest problems in AI services. A lot landed. But three questions kept surfacing across tables and nobody had a clean answer. We don't think anyone does yet. 1. How do you hire for judgment at scale? If AI commoditizes routine delivery and margin migrates to judgment, the staffing question becomes: how do you actually hire for the people who carry it? One table proposed a four-quadrant model — AI literacy × domain depth — where the overlap profiles command premium rates. The unresolved part: hiring for both halves at once is hard, and nobody had a recipe for doing it at scale. 2. How do you stop your key employees from becoming your competitors? This was the sharpest framing of the day. AI has collapsed the build cost of going independent — every senior engineer is now a latent competitor. The retention answer at the table was ownership, upside, and interesting work. But how much equity is enough to keep someone who can replicate the firm's output solo? Nobody could quantify it. 3. Encode your judgment into AI: durable moat, or slow leak of your edge? If SMEs are scarce and judgment is where margin sits, the obvious move is to encode that judgment into AI so the firm scales beyond the people who carry it. But the moment you do, you face the IP question: is this now a licensable asset you own, or did you just hand your edge to the model provider? Two founders raised this in the hallway after their session ended. Neither could resolve it then. Neither has since. These aren't rhetorical. They're the ones I'm still thinking about. If you have a take — genuinely interested. #ServiceAsSoftware #AIServices #VixulCon
